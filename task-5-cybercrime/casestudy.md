Phishing is a type of cybercrime in which attackers trick individuals into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, bank details, or OTPs by pretending to be a trusted entity. This is commonly done through fake emails, messages, or websites.

A typical phishing attack happens in several steps. First, the attacker creates a fake message that appears to come from a legitimate source, such as a bank or popular website. Next, the message includes a link that directs the victim to a fraudulent website designed to look real. When the victim enters their login credentials or financial details, the attacker captures this information. Finally, the attacker uses the stolen data for unauthorized transactions or identity misuse.

Students, elderly people, and individuals unfamiliar with cybersecurity practices are the most common targets. They may not recognize suspicious links or fake websites, making them more vulnerable.

The consequences of phishing can be severe. Victims may lose money from their bank accounts, have their social media or email accounts hacked, or suffer identity theft. In some cases, it can also damage a person’s reputation or academic or professional life.
